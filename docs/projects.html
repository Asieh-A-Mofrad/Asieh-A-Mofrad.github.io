Research

My work combines formal computational modelling with applied behavioural data science. The projects below show how that agenda travels across theory, experimentation, and real-world data.

Retail Fresh

Behavioural and context-aware modelling in retail systems

In the Retail Fresh project, I lead modelling work on retail demand and sales dynamics with the goal of supporting better forecasting and reducing food waste. The research combines machine learning with behavioural regularities in real purchasing environments, where weekly rhythms, holidays, local events, product-level patterns, and environmental factors all shape demand.

Retail Fresh is an externally funded Research Council of Norway R&D project with Link Retail as the project owner. My work is connected to the Machine Learning Group at UiB and focuses on how forecasting models can support operational decision making in food retail.

Role: Machine learning researcher and lead modeller
Methods: Forecasting, hierarchical time-series models, AutoML benchmarking, operational evaluation
Focus: Applied AI, interpretable forecasting, behavioural regularities in retail data

Current direction: Comparing direct, hierarchical, and automated forecasting approaches for bread demand and related retail categories, with attention to practical deployment and evaluation.

Associative Memory Models

Clique- and tournament-based associative memories

This line of work grows out of my PhD in Informatics and focuses on clique-based, nested-clique, and tournament-based associative memory architectures. These models are rooted in graph theory and coding theory, and they study how structured information can be stored, organised, and retrieved in scalable systems.

I am interested in associative memory not only as a neural-inspired mechanism, but as a way of thinking about representation itself: how information becomes structured, how sequences are preserved, and how retrieval can remain interpretable as systems scale.

Role: Researcher, model developer, proposal writer
Methods: Graph theory, coding theory, simulation, neural associative memory
Focus: Structured representation, sequence storage, theoretical AI

Current direction: Extending clique-tournament architectures and developing new proposals around memory structure and scalable recall.

Computational Behaviour and Projective Simulation

Adaptive learning, stimulus equivalence, and reinforcement

This research strand centres on computational models of adaptive behaviour, especially Projective Simulation, Equivalence Projective Simulation (EPS), and Enhanced-EPS. These models provide a computational lens on learning dynamics, generalisation, and the emergence of stimulus equivalence.

The same strand also includes work on contrastive reinforcement learning and on adaptive recommendation through the Balanced Difficulty Task Finder, where behavioural theory, user state, and feedback are used to calibrate challenge and engagement.

Role: PhD researcher, collaborator, supervisor
Methods: Reinforcement learning, projective simulation, contrastive learning, behavioural modelling
Focus: Learning dynamics, symbolic behaviour, adaptive systems

Current direction: New simulation frameworks that combine reinforcement and contrastive objectives, plus ongoing work on reject relations and quantum projective simulation.

Prescription History and Registry Data

Longitudinal prescription trajectories and Parkinson’s disease

As a postdoctoral researcher at UiB, and now through continued supervision and collaboration, I work with Norwegian prescription and patient registry data to study longitudinal trajectories associated with Parkinson’s disease and related outcomes.

These data are especially interesting because they are administrative at scale while also reflecting real-world decision processes over time. The modelling challenges include temporal representation, confounding, graph-based drug relationships, and interpretability in settings where domain understanding matters.

Role: Postdoctoral researcher, supervisor, collaborator
Methods: Representation learning, temporal modelling, graph-based modelling, longitudinal analysis
Focus: Health data, prescription trajectories, interpretable modelling

Current direction: Prescription-data pipelines, graph representations of drug histories, and supervised student projects on feature design, balancing strategies, and embedding-based analysis.

Digital Behaviour and NLP

Online discourse, digital traces, and responsible AI

My work on digital trace data explores how algorithms and online environments shape behaviour. This includes research on hate speech detection and discourse mapping in Norwegian social media, as well as broader interests in feedback loops, exposure dynamics, and responsible modelling in platform settings.

This area connects NLP workflows with behavioural data science and raises important questions about ethics, interpretability, and the long-term effects of adaptive systems.

Role: Assistant professor and researcher
Methods: NLP, BERT-based models, text analysis, digital trace analysis
Focus: Online behaviour, public discourse, responsible AI

Connecting Themes

Across these projects, a few ideas recur:

  • learning as adaptation under feedback
  • representation as a structured and interpretable object
  • behaviour as something that unfolds over time and context
  • machine learning as most useful when paired with theory, evaluation, and domain understanding

That combination of formal models and applied data work is the thread that ties together my academic profile.
